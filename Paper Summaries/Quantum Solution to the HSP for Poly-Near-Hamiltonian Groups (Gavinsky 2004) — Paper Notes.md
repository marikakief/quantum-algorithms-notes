## Metadata

- **Venue:** Quantum Information and Computation 4(3):229–235, 2004
- **Received / revised:** Received June 30, 2003; revised February 5, 2004
- **Algorithm Zoo entry:** #44
- **Input model:** finite group $G$ with oracle hiding a subgroup $H\leq G$; subgroups are represented by generating sets; subgroup membership is assumed efficient; the paper also assumes access to normal-HSP routines on certain generated subgroups.
- **Main parameter:**

$$
M_G := \bigcap_{K\leq G} N(K),\qquad k_G := [G:M_G],
$$

where $N(K)=\{g\in G: gKg^{-1}=K\}$ is the normalizer. Hamiltonian groups have all subgroups normal; abelian groups are a special case with $k_G=1$.

Here "Hamiltonian" is group-theoretic terminology for nonabelian groups in which every subgroup is normal. It is unrelated to Hamiltonian simulation.

---

## The computational problem

**Hidden subgroup problem (HSP):** given oracle access to a function $f:G\to S$ that is constant on each left coset of an unknown subgroup $H\leq G$ and takes distinct values on distinct left cosets, output generators for $H$.

Gavinsky studies HSP instances where the group is close to Hamiltonian in the sense that the intersection of all subgroup normalizers has small index. The primary theorem solves the HSP efficiently for **poly-near-Hamiltonian groups**, meaning

$$
[G:M_G] = \operatorname{poly}(n),\qquad n=\log |G|,
$$

under the same normal-HSP assumptions used by Grigni--Schulman--Vazirani--Vazirani. The final algorithm is stronger: it solves the case where

$$
[G:HM_G]\leq d=\operatorname{poly}(n),
$$

even if $[G:M_G]$ itself is not polynomially bounded.

---

## What the paper does

The paper turns a non-normal HSP instance into a small number of normal-HSP calls on subgroups built from cosets of $M_G$. The key observation is that $M_G$ normalizes every subgroup, so

$$
H\triangleleft HM_G.
$$

If one can identify the subgroup $HM_G/M_G\leq G/M_G$, then $HM_G$ is known as a union of cosets of $M_G$, and the remaining task is a normal HSP inside $HM_G$ because $H$ is normal in $HM_G$.

The contribution over Grigni--Schulman--Vazirani--Vazirani is the parameter range. GSVV handled $[G:M_G]\in \exp(O(\sqrt{\log n}))$ under assumptions including efficient QFT and representation-kernel intersection. Gavinsky pushes the efficient case to polynomial $[G:M_G]$, and then to polynomial $[G:HM_G]$ using random sampling rather than enumerating all of $G/M_G$.

## Algorithm / construction

### 1. Work modulo $M_G$

Because $M_G\triangleleft G$, elements of $G/M_G$ are cosets of $M_G$. For a subset $X\subseteq G/M_G$, Gavinsky writes

$$
U_{G/M_G}(X)=\bigcup_{x\in X} x\subseteq G,
$$

viewed as the subgroup of $G$ generated by the lifted cosets when $X$ generates a subgroup of $G/M_G$; it is not merely a set union unless the cosets already form a subgroup.

The goal is to build a set $T\subseteq G/M_G$ such that

$$
\langle T\rangle = HM_G/M_G.
$$

Then $U_{G/M_G}(T)=HM_G$, and a normal-HSP call on $HM_G$ recovers $H$.

### 2. Test whether a coset belongs to $HM_G/M_G$

For a candidate coset $x\in G/M_G$, set

$$
U=U_{G/M_G}(\langle x\rangle).
$$

Run the normal-HSP subroutine `NHS(U)` using the original oracle $f$ restricted to $U$.

- If $x\in HM_G/M_G$, then $H\cap U$ behaves as a normal subgroup of $U$ in the relevant generated subgroup, and `NHS(U)` returns useful generators. A nonempty intersection with the coset $x$ certifies that $x$ should be added to $T$.
- If $x\notin HM_G/M_G$, then the subroutine either fails or returns a subgroup whose intersection with $x$ is empty.

For the purely poly-near-Hamiltonian case, $|G/M_G|$ is polynomial, so one can sample from the returned subgroup and test membership in the coset to decide nonemptiness.

### 3. Enumeration algorithm for $[G:M_G]=\operatorname{poly}(n)$

Algorithm `HS_1(G)`:

1. Start with $T=\varnothing$.
2. Loop through all cosets $x\in G/M_G$.
3. Run `NHS(U_{G/M_G}(\langle x\rangle))`.
4. If the returned subgroup intersects the coset $x$, add $x$ to $T$.
5. Return `NHS(U_{G/M_G}(T))`.

The last call is valid because $U_{G/M_G}(T)=HM_G$ and $H\triangleleft HM_G$.

### 4. Sampling algorithm for $[G:HM_G]\leq d$

The more general algorithm `HS_2(G,d,\delta)` avoids enumerating $G/M_G$. It repeatedly samples random cosets $x\in G/M_G$ and applies the same membership test. As long as the generated subgroup $\langle T\rangle$ is still smaller than $HM_G/M_G$, a random sample lands in a useful new coset with probability at least $1/(2d)$. Each useful new coset at least doubles $|\langle T\rangle|$, so at most $n=\log |G|$ successful extensions are needed.

Gavinsky uses a Chernoff bound to show that

$$
4nd\ln(1/\delta)
$$

independent samples suffice for success probability at least $1-\delta$.

### 5. Coset-intersection check in the sampled setting

When $|G/M_G|$ may be large, sampling from a returned subgroup is no longer enough. Gavinsky uses normality of $M_G$:

$$
H_x\cap aM_G\neq\varnothing
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
 a\in H_xM_G.
$$

Since $H_xM_G$ is a subgroup generated by the union of known generators for $H_x$ and $M_G$, this reduces to the assumed subgroup-membership test.

## Key results

- **Efficient HSP for poly-near-Hamiltonian groups:** if $[G:M_G]=\operatorname{poly}(\log |G|)$ and the required normal-HSP calls are efficient, then HSP over $G$ is solved in quantum polynomial time.
- **More general indexed case:** if $[G:HM_G]\leq d=\operatorname{poly}(\log |G|)$, then `HS_2(G,d,\delta)` finds $H$ with probability at least $1-\delta$ using $O(nd\log(1/\delta))$ sampled cosets and polynomially many normal-HSP calls.
- **Assumption reduction:** the later algorithm does not need to iterate over all of $G/M_G$; it only requires uniform sampling from $G/M_G$ and efficient execution of the relevant normal-HSP subroutine.

## Comparison

- **Abelian HSP / Hamiltonian baseline:** abelian groups have $k_G=1$; Hamiltonian groups also make all subgroups normal. Gavinsky's setting quantifies distance from this normal-subgroup situation via $[G:M_G]$.
- **GSVV almost-abelian groups:** Grigni--Schulman--Vazirani--Vazirani used the same normalizer-intersection parameter but obtained efficiency for $[G:M_G]\in \exp(O(\sqrt{\log n}))$. Gavinsky extends the range to polynomial $[G:M_G]$ and then to polynomial $[G:HM_G]$.
- **[[Efficient Quantum Algorithms for Some Instances of the Non-Abelian HSP (Ivanyos-Magniez-Santha 2001) — Paper Notes|Ivanyos--Magniez--Santha (2001)]]:** IMS gives black-box group algorithms for normal HSP and other structured cases. Gavinsky treats such normal-HSP machinery as a subroutine.
- **[[From Optimal Measurement to Efficient Quantum Algorithms for the HSP over Semidirect Product Groups (Bacon-Childs-van Dam 2005) — Paper Notes|Bacon--Childs--van Dam (2005)]]:** later semidirect-product work designs explicit measurements. Gavinsky instead reduces to normal-HSP calls using normalizer structure.

## Limits / caveats

- The algorithm is conditional on efficient normal-HSP subroutines for the subgroups $U_{G/M_G}(\langle x\rangle)$ or $U_{G/M_G}(T)$, including efficient QFTs and representation-kernel intersection when using the standard normal-HSP algorithm.
- The paper assumes efficient subgroup membership for generated subgroups.
- The parameter $M_G$ must be usable algorithmically: one needs generators for $M_G$, coset handling in $G/M_G$, and either enumeration or uniform sampling from $G/M_G$ depending on the version.
- This is not a general solution to nonabelian HSP. The hard dihedral and symmetric-group HSPs are outside the guarantee unless their relevant index conditions and subroutines hold.
- OCR of the PDF has minor formula errors; the definitions above were normalized against the readable context and standard notation.

## Reusable ideas

1. **[[Normalizer-Core Reduction for Near-Hamiltonian HSP]]:** Intersect all normalizers to get a normal subgroup $M_G$ that normalizes every possible hidden subgroup. Then $H\triangleleft HM_G$, so recovering $HM_G/M_G$ reduces the original HSP to a normal-HSP call.
2. **[[Random Quotient Sampling to Generate Hidden Cosets]]:** If $[G:HM_G]$ is small, random cosets in $G/M_G$ hit useful extensions of $HM_G/M_G$ with inverse-polynomial probability; each accepted coset doubles the generated subgroup, giving a polynomial sample bound.
