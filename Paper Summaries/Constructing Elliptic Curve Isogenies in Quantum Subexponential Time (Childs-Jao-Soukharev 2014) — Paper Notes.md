> **Links:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/1012.4019) · [PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1012.4019)
> **Tags:** #paper #quantum-algorithms #hidden-shift #isogenies #post-quantum-cryptography #number-theory

## The computational problem

The paper studies the **horizontal isogeny construction problem** for ordinary elliptic curves over a finite field.

**Input.** A finite field $\mathbb F_q$, a discriminant $\Delta < 0$, and Weierstrass equations for two ordinary elliptic curves $E_0,E_1/\mathbb F_q$ such that:

- $E_0$ and $E_1$ are horizontally isogenous,
- $\operatorname{End}(E_0)=\operatorname{End}(E_1)=\mathcal O_\Delta$, an imaginary quadratic order,
- equivalently their $j$-invariants lie in
  $$
  \operatorname{Ell}_{q,n}(\mathcal O_\Delta),
  $$
  the set of isomorphism classes of curves over $\overline{\mathbb F}_q$ with endomorphism ring $\mathcal O_\Delta$ and fixed cardinality $n$.

**Output.** An ideal class $[\mathfrak s]\in \operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)$ such that
$$
[\mathfrak s] * j(E_0)=j(E_1),
$$
where
$$
* : \operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)\times \operatorname{Ell}_{q,n}(\mathcal O_\Delta)\to \operatorname{Ell}_{q,n}(\mathcal O_\Delta)
$$
is the isogeny star action. This ideal class specifies a horizontal isogeny up to isomorphism.

The complexity is measured in $\log q$ and $\log |\Delta|$, using the standard subexponential notation
$$
L_N(1/2,c)=\exp\!\left((c+o(1))\sqrt{\log N\log\log N}\right).
$$
The paper writes $L(c)$ for $L_{\max\{|\Delta|,q\}}(1/2,c)$.

---

## What the paper does

This is the first nontrivial use of [[A Subexponential-Time Quantum Algorithm for the Dihedral Hidden Subgroup Problem (Kuperberg 2005) — Paper Notes|Kuperberg's hidden-shift sieve]] in a concrete cryptanalytic setting. The reduction is clean: the free and transitive class-group action on horizontally isogenous curves turns isogeny construction into an **injective abelian hidden shift problem**.

The less flashy but technically important part is the oracle cost. A hidden-shift reduction is useless unless the hiding functions can be evaluated fast; the paper gives a subexponential algorithm for evaluating the isogeny star operator under GRH, avoiding the extra smoothness heuristics used in earlier isogeny algorithms. My assessment: the hidden-shift reduction is the conceptual result; the GRH expander/smoothing analysis is what makes the complexity claim defensible. "GRH-only" means only GRH beyond the usual computational model, not unconditional.

---

## The algorithm / construction

The full algorithm has two layers.

### 1. Evaluate the isogeny star operator

Given an ideal class $[\mathfrak b]\in \operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)$ and $j(E)\in \operatorname{Ell}_{q,n}(\mathcal O_\Delta)$, compute
$$
[\mathfrak b]*j(E).
$$

The paper does this by replacing $[\mathfrak b]$ with an equivalent product of small split-prime ideals.

**Factor base.** Choose split prime ideals
$$
\mathcal F=\{\mathfrak p_1,\ldots,\mathfrak p_f\}
$$
with norms up to $L(z)$, discarding primes dividing $qn$.

**Relation-finding.** Algorithm 1 samples random vectors $v\in \mathbb Z_{\ge 0}^f$ with $\|v\|_1=t$, where
$$
\frac{C\log |\operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)|}{\log\log |\Delta|}\le t\le C\log |\Delta|.
$$
For each $v$, it reduces an ideal in the class
$$
[\mathfrak b]\,[\mathcal F^v].
$$
If the reduced ideal has $\mathcal F$-smooth norm, Seysen's algorithm factors it over $\mathcal F$ as $\mathcal F^a$, and the algorithm returns
$$
z=a-v,
\qquad
[\mathfrak b]=[\mathcal F^z].
$$

The GRH input is the Jao--Miller--Venkatesan expansion theorem for the Cayley graph generated by small split primes. It says that short random walks in this class-group Cayley graph hit large enough subsets with nearly uniform probability. This replaces the heuristic claim that random reductions should behave like random ideals.

**Isogeny evaluation.** Once $[\mathfrak b]=[\mathcal F^z]$ with
$$
\|z\|_1 < (C+1)\log |\Delta|,
$$
Algorithm 2 composes the small-degree isogenies corresponding to the prime ideals in $\mathcal F^z$, using the Bröker--Charles--Lauter / Galbraith--Hess--Smart machinery plus Vélu-type formulas. The output is the $j$-invariant $j(E_c)$ satisfying
$$
j(E_c)=[\mathfrak b]*j(E).
$$

### 2. Reduce isogeny construction to injective abelian hidden shift

For the two input curves $E_0,E_1$, define functions
$$
f_c : \operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)\to \operatorname{Ell}_{q,n}(\mathcal O_\Delta),
\qquad
f_c([\mathfrak b])=[\mathfrak b]*j(E_c)
$$
for $c\in\{0,1\}$.

If $[\mathfrak s]$ is the quotient class with $[\mathfrak s]*j(E_0)=j(E_1)$, then
$$
f_1([\mathfrak b])=f_0([\mathfrak b][\mathfrak s]).
$$
The action is free and transitive, so $f_0$ is injective. This is exactly the injective hidden shift problem over the finite abelian group $\operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)$.

Algorithm 3 then:

1. Decomposes the class group as
   $$
   \operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)=\langle [\mathfrak b_1]\rangle\oplus\cdots\oplus \langle [\mathfrak b_k]\rangle,
   \qquad |\langle[\mathfrak b_j]\rangle|=n_j.
   $$
2. Runs an abelian hidden-shift algorithm on
   $$
   \mathbb Z_{n_1}\times\cdots\times\mathbb Z_{n_k}
   $$
   with hiding functions
   $$
   f_c(x_1,\ldots,x_k)=([\mathfrak b_1]^{x_1}\cdots[\mathfrak b_k]^{x_k})*j(E_c).
   $$
3. Returns
   $$
   [\mathfrak s]=[\mathfrak b_1]^{s_1}\cdots[\mathfrak b_k]^{s_k}.
   $$

Two hidden-shift back ends are analysed:

- [[A Subexponential-Time Quantum Algorithm for the Dihedral Hidden Subgroup Problem (Kuperberg 2005) — Paper Notes|Kuperberg (2005)]]: faster time, but $2^{O(\sqrt n)}$ quantum space.
- [[A Subexponential Time Algorithm for the Dihedral Hidden Subgroup Problem with Polynomial Space (Regev 2004) — Paper Notes|Regev (2004)]] extended here to arbitrary finite abelian groups: slower by an $L(\sqrt 2)$ factor, but polynomial space.

## Key results

### Isogeny star operator

**Theorem 2.1 / Corollary 2.2, used as the mixing input.** Under GRH, small split-prime ideal classes generate an expanding Cayley graph of $\operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)$ strongly enough that a random walk of length
$$
t\ge C\frac{\log |\operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)|}{\log\log |\Delta|}
$$
hits any subset $S$ with probability at least
$$
\frac12\frac{|S|}{|\operatorname{Cl}(\mathcal O_\Delta)|}.
$$
The corollary allows discarding primes dividing $qn$.

**Proposition 4.1.** Algorithm 1 runs in time
$$
L(z)+L\!\left(\frac{1}{4z}\right)
$$
under GRH.

**Proposition 4.3.** One loop iteration in Algorithm 1 produces an $\mathcal F$-smooth ideal with probability at least
$$
L\!\left(-\frac{1}{4z}\right).
$$

**Corollary 4.4.** With
$$
\ell=L\!\left(\frac{1}{4z}\right)
$$
trials, Algorithm 1 succeeds with probability at least
$$
1-\frac1e.
$$

**Proposition 4.5.** Any relation vector returned by Algorithm 1 satisfies
$$
\|z\|_1 < (C+1)\log |\Delta|.
$$

**Theorem 4.6.** Algorithm 2 succeeds with probability at least $1-1/e$ and runs in time
$$
L\!\left(\frac{1}{4z}\right)+\max\{L(3z),L(z)(\log q)^{3+\varepsilon}\}.
$$

**Corollary 4.7.** Choosing
$$
z=\frac{1}{2\sqrt 3}
$$
gives worst-case running time
$$
L_q\!\left(\frac12,\frac{\sqrt 3}{2}\right)
$$
for evaluating the isogeny star operator under GRH.

**Remark 4.8.** With Bisson's improved curve-evaluation method, this can improve to
$$
L_q\!\left(\frac12,\frac{1}{\sqrt 2}\right)
$$
for finding the target curve, though not for evaluating a normalised isogeny map.

### Hidden shift and isogeny construction

**Lemma 5.1.** The functions $f_0,f_1$ defined by the class-group action hide the shift $[\mathfrak s]$, and $f_0$ is injective:
$$
f_1([\mathfrak b])=f_0([\mathfrak b][\mathfrak s]).
$$

**Theorem 5.1, from [[A Subexponential-Time Quantum Algorithm for the Dihedral Hidden Subgroup Problem (Kuperberg 2005) — Paper Notes|Kuperberg]].** The abelian hidden shift problem has a quantum algorithm with time and query complexity
$$
2^{O(\sqrt n)}
$$
for finitely generated abelian groups, where $n$ is the output length. Here this contributes only $L(0)$ query overhead relative to the star-operator evaluation cost.

**Theorem 5.2.** For any finite abelian group $A$, hidden shift over $A$ can be solved in time and query complexity
$$
L_{|A|}\!\left(\frac12,\sqrt 2\right)
$$
using space $\operatorname{poly}(\log |A|)$.

**Theorem 5.4.** Under GRH, Algorithm 3 constructs a horizontal isogeny in time
$$
L_q\!\left(\frac12,\frac{\sqrt 3}{2}\right)
$$
when using Kuperberg's hidden-shift algorithm, and in time
$$
L_q\!\left(\frac12,\frac{\sqrt 3}{2}+\sqrt 2\right)
$$
when using the polynomial-space hidden-shift algorithm.

With Bisson's improvement (Remark 5.5), the corresponding constants become
$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt 2}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\frac{3}{\sqrt 2}.
$$

This result is about **ordinary** elliptic-curve isogenies, not the supersingular setting behind later SIDH/SIKE-style systems. Still, historically it changed the security picture for class-group-action cryptography: ordinary-isogeny schemes no longer had a clean story as quantum-resistant candidates.

## Limits / caveats

1. **GRH is still assumed.** The point is not unconditionality; it is that the runtime proof avoids the additional smoothness heuristics common in earlier isogeny work.

2. **The result is subexponential, not polynomial.** The bottlenecks are both the hidden-shift sieve and star-operator evaluation. Improving only one would not give a polynomial-time attack.

3. **Kuperberg space is large.** The better constant uses $2^{O(\sqrt n)}$ quantum space. The polynomial-space option costs the extra $L(\sqrt2)$ factor.

4. **Ordinary, horizontal isogenies only.** The paper explicitly does not handle supersingular isogeny problems. Do not read this as an attack on all isogeny-based cryptography.

5. **The algorithm returns an ideal-class description.** Recovering a normalised isogeny map can be done with the same asymptotic cost in their setting, but some later improvements for finding only the target curve do not directly apply to map evaluation.

6. **Cryptographic relevance is parameter-dependent.** The asymptotic result was damaging for ordinary-isogeny proposals, but the constants and quantum-space model matter for concrete attacks.
