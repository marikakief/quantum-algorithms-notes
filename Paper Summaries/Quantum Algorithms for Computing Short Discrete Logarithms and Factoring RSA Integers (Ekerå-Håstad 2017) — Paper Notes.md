# Quantum Algorithms for Computing Short Discrete Logarithms and Factoring RSA Integers (Ekerå-Håstad 2017) — Paper Notes

> **Links:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.00249) · [PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1702.00249)

## The computational problem

The paper treats three closely related problems.

**Short discrete logarithm.** Let $G=\langle g\rangle$ be a finite cyclic group of order $r$, written with operation $\odot$, and let

$$
x=[d]g
$$

with unknown integer $d$ satisfying $0<d<2^m$. Given $g$, $x$, and an efficient reversible implementation of the group operation, recover $d$. The order $r$ need not be known, but the analysis assumes enough headroom that no wraparound occurs in the main superposition:

$$
r \ge 2^{\ell+m}+2^\ell d .
$$

**RSA integer factoring.** Given $N=pq$ where $p$ and $q$ are random odd $n$-bit primes, recover $p$ and $q$.

**Order finding with side information.** Given a cyclic group $G=\langle g\rangle$ of order $r$ and an approximation $r_0$ with

$$
0\le r-r_0<2^m,
$$

recover $r$ by reducing to a short discrete logarithm.

The quantum cost measure is the size of the modular/group exponentiation in superposition. This is the right thing to count here: the arithmetic dominates the QFTs and the small-dimensional classical lattice post-processing.

---

## What the paper does

Ekerå and Håstad generalise Ekerå's short-discrete-log algorithm by splitting the information about $d$ across several smaller quantum runs and stitching the samples together classically with a lattice. For parameter $s\ge 1$ and $\ell\approx m/s$, the main quantum operation exponentiates to an exponent of length $\ell+m$ bits rather than the $2m$ bits needed in the one-shot version.

For RSA integers this turns factoring into a short discrete logarithm for

$$
d=\frac{p+q-2}{2},
$$

in the paper's original convention. Later [[How to Factor 2048 Bit RSA Integers in 8 Hours Using 20 Million Noisy Qubits (Gidney-Ekera 2021) — Paper Notes|Gidney--Ekerå]] uses the related convention $y=g^{N+1}$ and recovers $d=p+q$. Both reductions expose the same information after translating generators and targets, but the symbols should not be identified directly.

Thus an $n$-bit RSA modulus can be attacked with an exponent of length roughly $(1/2+1/s)n$ bits. Compared with [[Polynomial-Time Algorithms for Prime Factorization and Discrete Logarithms on a Quantum Computer (Shor 1994) — Paper Notes|Shor's factoring algorithm]], where the exponent length is $2n$, this is a serious resource reduction, though the paper gives asymptotic circuit-level savings rather than a full fault-tolerant resource estimate.

## The algorithm / construction

### Quantum sampling for short discrete logs

Fix $s\ge 1$ and choose $\ell$ close to $m/s$. The quantum stage takes $g$ and $x=[d]g$.

1. Prepare two index registers of lengths $\ell+m$ and $\ell$:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^{2\ell+m}}}
\sum_{a=0}^{2^{\ell+m}-1}\sum_{b=0}^{2^\ell-1}|a\rangle|b\rangle|0\rangle .
$$

2. Compute the group element

$$
[a]g\odot[-b]x=[a-bd]g
$$

into the third register:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^{2\ell+m}}}
\sum_a\sum_b |a,b,[a-bd]g\rangle .
$$

3. Apply a QFT of size $2^{\ell+m}$ to the first register and a QFT of size $2^\ell$ to the second register. The phase is

$$
\exp\left(\frac{2\pi i}{2^{\ell+m}}(aj+2^m bk)\right).
$$

4. Measure, obtaining a pair $(j,k)$ and an unused group element $[e]g$.

The constructive-interference condition is

$$
\left|\{dj+2^m k\}_{2^{\ell+m}}\right|\le 2^{m-2}.
$$

The paper calls such a pair **good**. For a good pair, $k$ is the $\ell$ high-order bits of $dj$ modulo $2^{\ell+m}$, up to the allowed error window.

### Classical lattice recovery from $s$ good pairs

Given distinct good pairs $(j_1,k_1),\ldots,(j_s,k_s)$, define the lattice $L\subset\mathbb Z^{s+1}$ generated by the row span of

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
j_1 & j_2 & \cdots & j_s & 1\\
2^{\ell+m} & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0\\
0 & 2^{\ell+m} & \cdots & 0 & 0\\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots\\
0 & 0 & \cdots & 2^{\ell+m} & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Also set

$$
\vec v=\bigl(\{-2^m k_1\}_{2^{\ell+m}},\ldots,\{-2^m k_s\}_{2^{\ell+m}},0\bigr).
$$

Search all lattice vectors $\vec u\in L$ satisfying

$$
\|\vec u-\vec v\| < \sqrt{s/4+1}\,2^m .
$$

For the true logarithm there are integers $m_i$ such that

$$
\vec u=\bigl(\{dj_1\}_{2^{\ell+m}}+m_1 2^{\ell+m},\ldots,\{dj_s\}_{2^{\ell+m}}+m_s2^{\ell+m},d\bigr)
$$

lies in this ball, because each good pair gives

$$
\left|\{dj_i+2^m k_i\}_{2^{\ell+m}}\right|\le 2^{m-2}.
$$

The final coordinate is tested by checking whether $x=[d]g$.

### RSA reduction

Let $N=pq$, with $2^{n-1}<p,q<2^n$. Work in a cyclic subgroup $G\le \mathbb Z_N^*$ generated by a random element $g$. Compute

$$
x=g^{(N-1)/2}.
$$

Since

$$
N-1=(p-1)+(q-1)+(p-1)(q-1),
$$

and $|G|=\varphi(N)/t$, the exponent satisfies

$$
\frac{N-1}{2}\equiv \frac{p+q-2}{2}\pmod{\varphi(N)/t}
$$

whenever $\varphi(N)/t>(p+q-2)/2$. Thus $x=g^d$ with

$$
d=\frac{p+q-2}{2},\qquad 2^n\le d<2^{n+1},
$$

so $m=n+1$. Once $d$ is recovered, set $c=d+1=(p+q)/2$ and solve

$$
p,q=c\pm\sqrt{c^2-N}.
$$

The short-log condition for the quantum algorithm becomes

$$
\frac{\varphi(N)}{t}\ge 2^{\ell+m}+2^\ell d,
$$

which the paper bounds by the sufficient condition

$$
t<2^{n-\ell-4}.
$$

For random RSA primes and a random cyclic subgroup, the authors argue this holds with overwhelming probability for $s>1$.

## Key results

**Good-pair count.** There are at least

$$
2^{\ell+m-1}
$$

values of $j$ for which some $k$ makes $(j,k)$ good.

**Per-pair probability.** Any fixed good pair $(j,k)$ is output by one execution with probability at least

$$
2^{-m-\ell-2}.
$$

Combining the two bounds, one execution outputs a good pair with probability at least $2^{-3}=1/8$.

**Main short-log theorem.** Let $0<d<2^m$, let $s\ge 1$ be fixed, let $\ell$ be close to $m/s$, and let $g$ generate a finite cyclic group of order

$$
r\ge 2^{\ell+m}+2^\ell d.
$$

Then there is a quantum algorithm that outputs a pair $(j,k)$ using, as its main operation, exponentiation of $g$ in superposition to an exponent of length $\ell+m$ bits. If the quantum stage is executed $O(s)$ times to produce a set $S$ of pairs, then a polynomial-time classical algorithm recovers $d$ by trying all unordered subsets of $s$ pairs from $S$.

**RSA exponent length.** For an $n$-bit RSA integer, $m=n+1$ and $\ell\approx (n+1)/s$, so the dominant exponent length is

$$
\ell+m\approx \left(1+\frac{1}{s}\right)(n+1).
$$

The paper phrases the RSA saving relative to the full $2n$-bit exponent in Shor's factoring algorithm as exponent length

$$
\left(\frac12+\frac1s\right)n
$$

when the modulus length is counted as $n$ bits. The convention shift is just whether $n$ denotes the prime length or the RSA modulus length.

## Comparison with prior work

| Method | Problem | Main quantum exponent length | Needs group order? | Comments |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| [[Polynomial-Time Algorithms for Prime Factorization and Discrete Logarithms on a Quantum Computer (Shor 1994) — Paper Notes|Shor factoring]] | factor an $n$-bit integer | $2n$ | no | General-purpose order finding. |
| Shor discrete log | DLP in group of order about $2^n$ | two $n$-bit exponents | yes/implicit group structure | Not tuned for short logs. |
| Ekerå short-DLP (2016) | $0<d<2^m$ | $2m$ and $m$ | no | One good sample suffices; recovered as the $s=1$ case here. |
| Ekerå-Håstad (this paper) | $0<d<2^m$ | $m+\ell$, with $\ell\approx m/s$ | no | Multiple short samples plus lattice stitching. |
| Ekerå-Håstad RSA factoring | RSA semiprime | about $(1/2+1/s)n$ for an $n$-bit modulus | no | Reduces factoring to a short DLP for $(p+q-2)/2$. |

My assessment: the paper's core contribution is not a new hidden-subgroup problem; it is a resource tradeoff for the same Fourier-sampling physics. The interesting part is that the classical lattice step lets you cut the quantum exponent register below the one-shot short-log size.

## Limits / caveats

- The parameter $s$ is treated as a fixed constant. If $s$ grows, the subset search and lattice enumeration constants stop being harmless.
- The short-DLP theorem assumes

  $$
  r\ge 2^{\ell+m}+2^\ell d,
  $$

  so the superposition does not wrap modulo $r$. This is exactly why the RSA reduction has to argue that the chosen cyclic subgroup is large enough.
- The RSA algorithm is probabilistic through the random subgroup generator. The paper gives a high-probability argument for random RSA primes and $s>1$, not a worst-case guarantee for every semiprime and every chosen base.
- The paper counts exponent length, not full Toffoli/T-depth, qubit layout, fault-tolerant overhead, or modular-multiplication architecture. For concrete costing, pair it with [[How to Factor 2048 Bit RSA Integers in 8 Hours Using 20 Million Noisy Qubits (Gidney-Ekera 2021) — Paper Notes|Gidney--Ekerå (2021)]].
- The classical post-processing assumes enough good pairs. The lower bound gives success probability at least $1/8$ per run for a good pair, but this is conservative and does not tightly model practical sampling.
