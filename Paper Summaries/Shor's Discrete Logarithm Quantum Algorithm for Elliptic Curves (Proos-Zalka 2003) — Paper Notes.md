> **Links:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/0301141) · [PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/quant-ph/0301141)

## The computational problem

The paper implements [[Polynomial-Time Algorithms for Prime Factorization and Discrete Logarithms on a Quantum Computer (Shor 1994) — Paper Notes|Shor's discrete-log algorithm]] for elliptic curves over $\mathrm{GF}(p)$, with $p>3$ prime.

**Input.** An elliptic curve

$$
E: y^2 = x^3 + ax + b \pmod p,
$$

with $4a^3+27b^2 \neq 0$, a point $P\in E(\mathrm{GF}(p))$ of known prime order $q$, and a point $Q=dP$ in the cyclic subgroup generated by $P$.

**Output.** The discrete logarithm $d\in\{0,\ldots,q-1\}$ such that $Q=dP$.

**Complexity measure.** The paper counts qubits and arithmetic gates for a reversible circuit. It measures time mostly in $n$-bit additions, where $n\approx \log_2 p\approx \log_2 q$.

The finite-field case covered is prime-field ECC only. Curves over $\mathrm{GF}(2^m)$ are explicitly left out.

---

## What the paper does

This is a gate-level implementation study for elliptic-curve discrete logs, not a new asymptotic algorithm. The result is still worth documenting: Proos and Zalka show that Shor's ECDLP algorithm can be run with $O(n)$ logical qubits and $O(n^3)$ gates, with concrete estimates around $1000$ qubits for a 160-ish-bit ECC key.

The technical core is not Fourier sampling — that is inherited from [[Polynomial-Time Algorithms for Prime Factorization and Discrete Logarithms on a Quantum Computer (Shor 1994) — Paper Notes|Shor]]. The hard part is reversible prime-field inversion inside elliptic-curve addition, handled by a desynchronised reversible extended Euclidean algorithm.

My assessment: this is an early resource-estimation paper in spirit. The constants are pre-surface-code and pre-Toffoli-count culture, so they are not directly comparable to modern estimates, but the circuit ideas — semiclassical QFT register recycling, in-place point shifts, reversible EEA scheduling — are still the right conceptual pieces.

## The algorithm / construction

### 1. Fourier-sampling formulation of ECDLP

For a cyclic group $\langle P\rangle$ of prime order $q$, write $Q=dP$. The function sampled by Shor's discrete-log algorithm is

$$
f(x,y)=xP+yQ=(x+dy)P.
$$

Over $\mathbb{Z}_q^2$, the hidden period lattice is generated by

$$
(q,0),\qquad (d,-1),
$$

since

$$
f(x+q,y)=f(x,y),\qquad f(x+d,y-1)=f(x,y).
$$

If one could apply QFTs of exact order $q$, the post-QFT measurement would give pairs $(x',y')$ satisfying

$$
y'\equiv d x' \pmod q,
$$

so, for $x'\neq 0$,

$$
d\equiv y'(x')^{-1}\pmod q.
$$

The implemented circuit instead uses the standard $2^n$-point [[Quantum Fourier Transform Circuit]], with $N=2^n\approx q$. Appendix A analyses the usual smeared peaks: after scaling measured $(x',y')$ by $q/N$ and rounding, plus trying nearby classical candidates, one obtains a high constant / large success probability under the paper's extra-bit and post-processing choices. Increasing $n$ beyond $\log_2 q$ decreases this Fourier-rounding failure exponentially, but "one run succeeds with probability close to 1" is convention-dependent rather than a bare theorem statement.

### 2. Semiclassical QFT eliminates the $x,y$ registers

Naively, the state preparation would be

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{x,y=0}^{N-1} |x,y,0\rangle
\mapsto
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{x,y=0}^{N-1} |x,y,xP+yQ\rangle.
$$

Proos and Zalka use the Griffiths--Niu semiclassical QFT idea, also used in small-space Shor implementations, to avoid storing the $x$ and $y$ registers. Process bits from high to low significance:

1. Prepare one control qubit in $(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$.
2. Use it to conditionally apply a fixed elliptic-curve group shift by $2^iP$ or $2^iQ$.
3. Measure the control qubit in the basis required by the semiclassical QFT, with later bases depending on earlier measurement outcomes.

Thus the live quantum data is essentially the elliptic-curve accumulator plus arithmetic work space, not two full Fourier input registers. See [[Semiclassical QFT Register Recycling for Abelian Period Finding]].

### 3. Point multiplication becomes $2n$ fixed group shifts

The classically known points

$$
P_i=2^iP,
\qquad
Q_i=2^iQ
$$

are precomputed. The quantum circuit applies $n$ controlled shifts by $P_i$ and $n$ controlled shifts by $Q_i$.

For a fixed point $A=(\alpha,\beta)$, a shift acts as

$$
U_A: |S\rangle\mapsto |S+A\rangle.
$$

The paper avoids handling all elliptic-curve addition cases exactly. It randomly initialises the accumulator to $kP\neq O$ and then uses only the generic addition formula. The exceptional states $S=A$, $S=-A$, and $S=O$ occupy tiny mass on average; the expected fidelity loss over $2n$ shifts is

$$
O\!\left(\frac{n}{q}\right),
$$

more explicitly about $4n/q$ in their estimate. See [[Random-Offset Generic Elliptic-Curve Group Shifts]].

### 4. In-place generic elliptic-curve group shift

For $S=(x,y)$ and $A=(\alpha,\beta)$ in the generic case,

$$
\lambda = \frac{y-\beta}{x-\alpha},
\qquad
x'=\lambda^2-(x+\alpha),
\qquad
 y'=\lambda(x-x')-y.
$$

The useful reversible decomposition is

$$
(x,y)
\leftrightarrow (x,\lambda)
\leftrightarrow (x',\lambda)
\leftrightarrow (x',y').
$$

Equivalently, using the inverse expression

$$
\lambda = \frac{-y'+\beta}{x'-\alpha},
$$

one can compute and then uncompute the slope without a second full output copy. One group shift needs two divisions of the form

$$
(x,y)\leftrightarrow (x,y/x),
$$

one multiplication for $\lambda^2$, and a few modular additions/subtractions. The divisions are the expensive part. See [[Slope-Uncomputed In-Place Elliptic-Curve Shifts]].

### 5. Division through reversible modular inversion

A division $x,y\leftrightarrow x,y/x$ is decomposed as

$$
(x,y)
\xleftrightarrow{E}
(x^{-1},y)
\xleftrightarrow{m}
(x^{-1},y,y/x)
\xleftrightarrow{E}
(x,y,y/x)
\xleftrightarrow{m}
(x,0,y/x),
$$

where $E$ is a reversible extended Euclidean algorithm computing $x^{-1}\bmod p$, and $m$ denotes modular multiplication or its inverse.

Modular multiplication of two quantum registers is done by the schoolbook expansion

$$
xy \equiv \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} x_i 2^i y \pmod p,
$$

implemented through $n$ rounds of modular doubling and conditional modular addition. The paper estimates about $3n$ additions per modular multiplication.

### 6. Desynchronised reversible extended Euclidean algorithm

The extended Euclidean algorithm maintains two pairs

$$
(a,A),\qquad (b,B),
$$

starting from

$$
(a,A)=(0,p),\qquad (b,B)=(1,x),
$$

with $A\equiv ax\pmod p$ and $B\equiv bx\pmod p$. It terminates with one pair $(\pm p,0)$ and the other $(x^{-1},1)$ or $(x^{-1}-p,1)$.

A Euclidean iteration is reversible because the quotient can be uncomputed from the updated coefficient registers. If

$$
(a,A),(b,B)\mapsto (a-qb,A-qB),(b,B),
$$

with $q=\lfloor A/B\rfloor$, then $q$ can be recovered from the modified coefficient data. If $a'=a-qb$ is the updated first coefficient and the paper's sign/order invariant holds, then

$$
q=\left\lfloor \frac{-a'}{b}\right\rfloor.
$$

This is the source of stepwise reversibility: the quotient is computed from the second components before the update but uncomputed from the first components after the update, so it need not be stored through the whole inverse-finding routine.

The problem is variable running time: different basis states need different quotients and a different number of Euclidean iterations. Proos and Zalka handle this by allowing different branches of the superposition to progress at different logical speeds. Each branch carries a small control register saying which of five operations it currently needs:

1. grow a shift counter until $2^iB>A$;
2. compute quotient bits from high to low by conditional subtraction;
3. update the coefficient register using those quotient bits;
4. uncompute the shift counter;
5. swap the Euclidean pairs so the larger second coordinate comes first.

The physical circuit cycles through these five operations, each conditioned on the branch's control bits. A branch that does not need the current operation is left unchanged. See [[Desynchronised Reversible Euclidean Algorithm]].

Because a reversible computation cannot simply stop in one branch, completed branches increment a small completion counter during remaining cycles. This leaves a small amount of garbage, but the division routine later runs the Euclidean computation backward, so the garbage is acceptable.

## Key results

### Fourier sampling for ECDLP

With exact QFTs over $\mathbb{Z}_q$, a measurement yields

$$
(x',y')=(k,dk)\pmod q
$$

for uniformly random $k\in\mathbb{Z}_q$. If $k\neq 0$, then

$$
d\equiv y'(x')^{-1}\pmod q.
$$

With $N=2^n$ QFTs, the measured values are sampled from sinc-like peaks centred at

$$
\left(\frac{Nk}{q},\frac{Ndk}{q}\right).
$$

Rounding after scaling by $q/N$, plus checking nearby candidates, gives success probability close to $1$ for a single noiseless run.

### Euclidean-cycle bound

Let $q_1,\ldots,q_r$ be the quotients in the Euclidean algorithm on $(p,x)$, and define the number of five-operation cycles as

$$
t(p,x)=r+4\sum_{i=1}^r \lfloor \log_2 q_i\rfloor.
$$

For coprime $p>x\geq 1$ with $p>2$, Appendix B proves

$$
t(p,x)\leq 4.5\log_2 p.
$$

The average is experimentally and analytically around $3.5n$ cycles, but the circuit uses the $4.5n$ worst-case bound.

### Reversible EEA complexity

One reversible modular inversion via their EEA schedule uses

$$
O(n^2)
$$

time and

$$
O(n)
$$

space. More concretely, the Euclidean subroutine needs about

$$
5n+4\log_2 n+\epsilon
$$

qubits without register sharing, or

$$
3n+8\sqrt n+4\log_2 n+\epsilon
$$

with the proposed register-sharing heuristic.

### Full ECDLP circuit complexity

The full ECDLP algorithm uses $2n$ fixed group shifts. Each shift uses two divisions, one multiplication, and additions. The paper's total time estimate is:

Without register sharing,

$$
T\approx 360 n^2
$$

$n$-bit classical-quantum additions, hence about

$$
O(n^3)
$$

basic gates.

With register sharing,

$$
T\approx 205n^2+615n^{3/2}
$$

$n$-bit classical-quantum additions, again $O(n^3)$ gates.

The maximum qubit count is

$$
f(n)=7n+4\log_2 n+\epsilon
$$

without register sharing, and

$$
f'(n)=5n+8\sqrt n+4\log_2 n+\epsilon
$$

with register sharing.

For the comparison table in the paper, a 163-bit ECC discrete-log instance is estimated at about $1000$ qubits with register sharing, versus about $2048$ qubits for the security-comparable 1024-bit RSA factoring instance under the then-best small-space factoring estimate.

## Limits / caveats

- **Only prime-field curves.** The implementation is for $\mathrm{GF}(p)$, not binary fields $\mathrm{GF}(2^m)$ or extension fields.
- **Known prime order.** They assume $P$ has known prime order $q$. They explain the standard reductions for composite order, but the resource estimates target the prime-order cryptographic case.
- **Not a fault-tolerant resource estimate.** The paper counts logical qubits and addition-like operations, not $T$ gates, surface-code cycles, routing overhead, or magic-state factories.
- **Exceptional elliptic-curve additions are treated probabilistically.** The generic addition formula ignores rare cases and loses about $4n/q$ expected fidelity. This is fine asymptotically and for large cryptographic groups, but it is an approximation choice.
- **Bounded quotients trade fidelity for space.** Bounding Euclidean quotients to $3\log_2 n$ bits gives estimated total fidelity loss at most $12/n$ across the ECDLP algorithm. That is not negligible at small toy sizes.
- **Register sharing is partly heuristic.** The $5n+8\sqrt n+4\log_2 n+\epsilon$ space estimate uses empirical size-perturbation behaviour of Euclidean runs.
- **Parallelism is not optimised.** They explicitly do not try to parallelise; later ECC resource papers are better for depth estimates.

## Reusable ideas

1. [[Semiclassical QFT Register Recycling for Abelian Period Finding]] — replace a large Fourier input register by one measured control qubit reused across controlled powers/shifts.
2. [[Random-Offset Generic Elliptic-Curve Group Shifts]] — randomise the accumulator so exceptional group-addition cases carry tiny expected amplitude, letting the circuit implement only the generic formula.
3. [[Slope-Uncomputed In-Place Elliptic-Curve Shifts]] — implement $S\mapsto S+A$ by computing the slope, updating $x$, then using the inverse slope formula to uncompute into $y'$.
4. [[Desynchronised Reversible Euclidean Algorithm]] — let superposed branches of a reversible variable-time arithmetic subroutine progress at different speeds using local control flags.
