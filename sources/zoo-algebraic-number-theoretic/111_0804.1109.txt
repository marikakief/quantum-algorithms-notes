Abstract. We discuss classical and quantum algorithms for solvabil-
ity testing and ’¼ünding integer solutions x, y of equations of the form
af x + bgy = c over ’¼ünite ’¼üelds Fq. A quantum algorithm with time com-
plexity q3/8(log q)O(1) is presented. While still superpolynomial in log q,
this quantum algorithm is signi’¼ücantly faster than the best known classi-
cal algorithm, which has time complexity q9/8(log q)O(1). Thus it gives an
example of a natural problem where quantum algorithms provide about
a cubic speed-up over classical ones.

Connection with the Hidden Subgroup Problem
The pretty good measurement approach [1] to the Hidden Subgroup Prob-
lem (hsp) over the non-abelian group Z/q ŌŗŖZ/p with q a prime and
q/p2 = (log q)O(1) shows that the hsp can be solved e’¼āciently on a quan-
tum computer if one can e’¼āciently solve the equation af x + bf y = c,
where f has multiplicative order p in Z/q. All algorithms presented in
this article have superpolynomial complexity in log q and hence fall short
of this goal.
For this restricted problem with f = g and f of order p ŌēłŌłÜq, there
are p2 possible solutions (x, y), hence even a classical algorithm has OŌłŚ(q)
time complexity instead of the OŌłŚ(q9/8) of Theorem 1. Quantum mechan-
ically, one can ŌĆśGrover searchŌĆÖ the set of solutions x Ōłł{0, . . . , p ŌłÆ1} in
time OŌłŚ(q1/4), which, although better than the OŌłŚ(q3/8) of Theorem 3, is
still far from polynomial in log q.

Remarks and Open Problems
We remark that in some ’¼ünite ’¼üelds classical subexponential probabilis-
tic algorithms are possible for the discrete logarithm problem, see [4,
Section 6.4]. In such ’¼üelds, a version of Theorem 1 can be obtained with
an algorithm that runs in probabilistic time q3/4+o(1), which is still much
slower that the quantum algorithm of Theorems 3 and 4. We note that al-
though over the last several years fast heuristic algorithms for the discrete
logarithm problem have been designed to work over any ’¼ünite ’¼üeld, rig-
orous subexponential algorithms are know only for ’¼üelds of special types
(such as prime ’¼üelds Fp or binary ’¼üelds F2m), see [4, Section 6.4] for more
details. Clearly using probabilistic algorithms one can also get additional
speed up in the classical case if the multiplicative orders s and t are large
(similar to Theorems 4 and 6).
To try to strengthen the presented results one can consider the ana-
logue to Equation (1) for elliptic curves E over Fq. For example, given
two Fq-rational points F, G ŌłłE(Fq) and the values a, b, c ŌłłFq one can
ask for solutions to the equation
a ┬Ę x([u]F) + b ┬Ę x([v]G) = c
in integers u and v (where x(Q) denotes the x-coordinate of a point
Q ŌłłE(Fq) in a ’¼üxed a’¼āne model of E and [n]Q denotes the n-fold sum
Q ŌŖĢQ ŌŖĢ┬Ę ┬Ę ┬Ę ŌŖĢQ in the group of E). Using bounds of character sums over
subgroups of elliptic curves, see [7], one can obtain full analogues of our


results (in fact at the cost of only typographical changes). This case is
interesting since in the classical scenario even heuristic subexponential
algorithms are not known.
But above of this all, it still remains an open problem whether or not
there exist e’¼ācient quantum algorithms that run in time (log q)O(1) for
the determining the integer solutions x, y to the equation af x + bgy = c
and even the more restricted version af x + bf y = c over Fq.
