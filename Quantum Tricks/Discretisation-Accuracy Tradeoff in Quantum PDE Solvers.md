# Discretisation-Accuracy Tradeoff in Quantum PDE Solvers

> **Source:** Montanaro-Pallister, arXiv:1512.05903
> **Tags:** #trick #QLSA #PDE #finite-element-method #complexity-analysis

## What it does
Exposes a hidden relationship between system size and accuracy in quantum algorithms for discretised PDEs, which can reduce an apparent exponential speedup to a polynomial one.

## The trick
When a PDE is discretised (FEM, FDM, etc.), the system size $N$ is chosen to achieve a target accuracy $\epsilon$. For a $d$-dimensional domain with degree-$k$ basis functions:

$$N = O\!\left(\left(\frac{|u|_{k+1}}{\epsilon}\right)^{d/(k+1)}\right)$$

A quantum algorithm that is "exponentially faster in $N$" actually has runtime that depends on $\epsilon$ too:

- **Quantum:** $\tilde{O}(\text{polylog}(N) \cdot \text{poly}(\kappa, 1/\epsilon))$ for producing the state, plus $O(1/\epsilon)$ for measurement
- **Classical:** $O(N \cdot \text{poly}(\kappa, \log 1/\epsilon))$

The exponential advantage in $N$ can be partially or fully cancelled by the $\text{poly}(1/\epsilon)$ measurement cost. The net speedup depends on $d$: for fixed $d$, it's polynomial; for growing $d$, it can be exponential.

**Checklist for any quantum PDE algorithm:**
1. What is $N(\epsilon)$?
2. What is $\kappa(N)$?
3. What is the measurement/extraction cost?
4. Substitute (1) and (2) into the quantum runtime and compare with classical.

## When to reach for it
- Evaluating claimed quantum speedups for PDE solvers
- Any QLSA application where the linear system is generated by discretisation
- Comparing quantum and classical algorithms for scientific computing

## Complexity
Not a computational trick — it's an analysis framework. The key insight is bookkeeping: express everything in terms of $\epsilon$ and $d$, not $N$.

## Caveat
The tradeoff depends on the discretisation scheme. Adaptive methods (hp-FEM) can achieve $\|u - \tilde{u}\| = O(e^{-1/h})$, which changes the $N(\epsilon)$ relationship and could restore an exponential advantage. Also, for high-dimensional problems where $N$ grows exponentially in $d$, the quantum advantage can be genuine.
