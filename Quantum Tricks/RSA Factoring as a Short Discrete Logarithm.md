# RSA Factoring as a Short Discrete Logarithm

> **Source:** Martin Ekerå and Johan Håstad, arXiv:1702.00249
> **Tags:** #trick #factoring #discrete-log #cryptanalysis

## What it does

Turns factoring an RSA semiprime into recovering the short logarithm $(p+q-2)/2$ in a subgroup of $\mathbb Z_N^*$.

## The trick

Let $N=pq$ with similarly sized odd primes. In a cyclic subgroup $G\le \mathbb Z_N^*$ generated by $g$, compute

$$
x=g^{(N-1)/2}.
$$

Since

$$
N-1=(p-1)+(q-1)+(p-1)(q-1),
$$

we have, modulo a large enough subgroup order,

$$
\frac{N-1}{2}\equiv \frac{p+q-2}{2}.
$$

Thus $x=g^d$ for

$$
d=\frac{p+q-2}{2},
$$

which is roughly half the bit length of $N$. A short-discrete-log algorithm recovers $d$. Then

$$
c=d+1=\frac{p+q}{2},\qquad p,q=c\pm\sqrt{c^2-N}.
$$

## When to reach for it

Use it when factoring semiprimes with balanced factors and when the goal is to reduce the quantum exponentiation width relative to textbook [[Order-Finding via QFT and Continued Fractions]].

## Complexity

For an $n$-bit RSA modulus, Ekerå-Håstad's multi-sample short-log routine uses a main exponent length about

$$
\left(\frac12+\frac1s\right)n
$$

for fixed $s>1$, compared with $2n$ in the standard Shor factoring setup.

## Caveat

The subgroup must be large enough: in the paper's notation, if $|G|=\varphi(N)/t$, a sufficient condition is

$$
t<2^{n-\ell-4}
$$

when the prime factors have $n$ bits. Random choices satisfy this with high probability in the authors' model, but this is not a deterministic guarantee for every base.
