# Quantum Walk Simulation of Non-Unitary Dynamics

## What it does

Simulates the non-unitary evolution $e^{\mathcal{L}t}$ generated by a non-Hermitian operator $\mathcal{L}$ (such as a Lindbladian or a decay Hamiltonian) by embedding it in a larger unitary system and applying a quantum-walk-based Hamiltonian simulation to the extended space. This avoids the need for product formulas on non-Hermitian generators, which can fail to preserve positivity or trace.

## The trick

Given a completely positive trace-preserving map with generator $\mathcal{L}$, construct a Stinespring dilation: there exists a unitary $U$ on $\mathcal{H} \otimes \mathcal{H}_E$ (system plus environment) such that

$$e^{\mathcal{L}t}(\rho) = \text{Tr}_E[U(t) (\rho \otimes |0\rangle\langle 0|) U(t)^\dagger]$$

The generator of $U(t)$ is a Hermitian Hamiltonian $\tilde{H}$ on $\mathcal{H} \otimes \mathcal{H}_E$. When $\mathcal{L}$ acts on $N$-dimensional space with $K$ Lindblad operators, $\tilde{H}$ acts on $O(NK)$-dimensional space and has spectral norm $O(\|\mathcal{L}\|^{1/2})$ (the square root appears because $\mathcal{L}$ is the generator of a contraction semigroup and $\tilde{H}$ is chosen so that $\tilde{H}^2 \approx -\mathcal{L}$).

The quantum walk proceeds by applying the walk operator $W = (2\Pi_s - I)(2\Pi_e - I)$ where $\Pi_s$ projects onto the space of valid state-environment pairs and $\Pi_e$ is the prepare-environment projector. The eigenvalues of $W$ are $e^{\pm i\arcsin(\lambda_j^{1/2})}$ where $\lambda_j$ are the eigenvalues of $\mathcal{L}$, enabling eigenvalue readout and time evolution via phase estimation on $W^t$.

Key formula from [[Efficient Simulation of Sparse Markovian Quantum Dynamics (Childs-Li 2017) — Paper Notes|Childs-Li 2017]] (adapting [[Quantum Speed-Up of Markov Chain Based Algorithms (Szegedy 2004) — Paper Notes|Szegedy 2004]] and [[Exponential Improvement in Precision for Simulating Sparse Hamiltonians (Berry-Childs-Cleve-Kothari-Somma 2014) — Paper Notes|Berry et al. 2014]]): If $\|\tilde{H}\| \leq \Lambda^{1/2}$ (where $\Lambda = \|\mathcal{L}\|$), then simulating $e^{\mathcal{L}t}$ to error $\epsilon$ costs $O(\Lambda^{1/2} t^{1/2} / \epsilon^{1/2})$ applications of the walk operator and its inverse.

The square-root dependence on $\Lambda$ and $t$ is the hallmark of the quantum walk speedup: it corresponds to $O(\sqrt{T})$ steps of a random walk to mix in time $T$.

## When to reach for it

- Simulating Lindblad master equations without assuming that individual jump operators commute or that a product formula applies.
- Any open-system simulation where the Lindbladian is given via sparse oracles rather than as a local or geometrically structured operator.
- Computing steady states of Lindbladians: the steady state corresponds to the $\lambda = 0$ eigenstate of $\mathcal{L}$, accessible by quantum phase estimation on $W$.
- Simulating classical Markov chains on a quantum computer ([[Quantum Speed-Up of Markov Chain Based Algorithms (Szegedy 2004) — Paper Notes|Szegedy 2004]]): this is the foundational use case that the Lindblad algorithm generalizes.

## Complexity

For an $N$-dimensional Lindblad system with $K$ jump operators, sparsity $d$, and Lindbladian spectral norm $\Lambda$:

- Environment size: $O(K \log d)$ additional qubits
- Walk operator cost: $O(d^2 K)$ oracle queries per application
- Total simulation to error $\epsilon$ in time $t$: $\tilde{O}(d^2 K \cdot \Lambda^{1/2} t^{1/2} / \epsilon^{1/2})$ queries

Compare with Hamiltonian simulation: $\tilde{O}(d \cdot \|H\| t / \epsilon)$ or $\tilde{O}(d \cdot \|H\| t)$ (with QSP). The Lindblad cost has better $\epsilon$-dependence ($\epsilon^{-1/2}$ vs $\epsilon^{-1/3}$ or better for Hamiltonian simulation in some regimes) but worse $t$-scaling relative to some methods.

## Caveat

- The $t^{1/2}$ query scaling is not obviously optimal for Lindblad simulation; it comes from the walk, which maps $\Lambda$ to $\Lambda^{1/2}$ via the square-root dilation. Whether the true complexity is $t^1$ or $t^{1/2}$ is open.
- The dilation requires coherent access to the environment register; this adds qubit overhead and is nontrivial to implement fault-tolerantly.
- The walk approach simulates the evolution of the density matrix as a quantum state; extracting expectation values still requires shadow tomography or projective measurement with $O(1/\epsilon^2)$ repetitions unless the observable has special structure.
- The approach does not directly generalize to time-dependent Lindbladians without additional work.
