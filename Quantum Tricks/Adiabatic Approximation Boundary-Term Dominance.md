# Adiabatic Approximation Boundary-Term Dominance

> **Source:** Cheung, Høyer, Wiebe, arXiv:1103.4174; also noted in Jansen-Ruskai-Seiler 2007, MMP 2006, Sun 1988
> **Tags:** #trick #adiabatic #integration-by-parts #asymptotic-expansion #boundary-terms

## What it does

Shows that the leading $O(1/T)$ term in the adiabatic approximation error depends only on the Hamiltonian at the **endpoints** $s = 0$ and $s = 1$, not on the interior of the path. The interior contributions cancel by phase oscillation in the large-$T$ limit.

## The trick

Apply integration by parts (IBP) to the one-jump path integral:

$$\int_0^1 \beta_{\nu,G}(s) e^{-i\int_0^s \gamma_{G,\nu}(\xi)d\xi \cdot T} ds = \left[\frac{\beta_{\nu,G}(s) e^{-i\int_0^s \gamma_{G,\nu}(\xi)d\xi \cdot T}}{-i\gamma_{G,\nu}(s)T}\right]_0^1 - \int_0^1 \frac{\partial_s[\beta_{\nu,G}(s)/(-i\gamma_{G,\nu}(s)T)]} \cdot e^{-i\int_0^s \gamma_{G,\nu} \cdot T} ds$$

The **boundary term** (first term) is $O(1/T)$. The **remainder integral** is $O(1/T^2)$ (apply IBP again to see this).

Summing over all states $|\nu \neq G\rangle$, the leading-order error is:

$$\sum_{\nu \neq G} e^{-i\phi_\nu}|\nu(1)\rangle \left[\frac{\langle\dot\nu(s)|G(s)\rangle e^{-i\int_0^s \gamma_{G,\nu}\cdot T}}{-i\gamma_{G,\nu}(s)T}\right]_{s=0}^{s=1} + O(1/T^2)$$

The sum at $s=1$ depends on $\dot H(1)$, $\gamma(1)$; the sum at $s=0$ depends on $\dot H(0)$, $\gamma(0)$. **Nothing from the middle.**

**Physical intuition:** In the large-$T$ limit, paths jumping at interior times accumulate vastly different phases depending on exactly when the jump occurs. They tend toward uniform distribution on the unit circle and cancel. Only the very first and very last "incomplete oscillations" — which depend on $H(0)$ and $H(1)$ — do not average out.

## When to reach for it

- **Designing adiabatic schedules:** If you want to suppress the $O(1/T)$ error, the target is $\dot H(0)$ and $\dot H(1)$ — make the Hamiltonian's rate of change vanish at the endpoints. This is exactly why smooth "turn-on/turn-off" schedules (start and end with $\dot s = 0$) work better.
- **Proving tighter error bounds:** After IBP, the leading term is explicit and the remainder is one order smaller. Apply IBP again for $O(1/T^2)$ bounds.
- **Explaining why error suppression is possible:** The $O(1/T^2)$ timing windows in the search Hamiltonian (Eq. 71 of the paper) arise precisely because the boundary contributions cancel for specific $T$ values.

## Complexity

Leading error $O(\|\dot H(0)\| / \gamma(0)^2 T + \|\dot H(1)\| / \gamma(1)^2 T)$. Remainder $O(1/T^2)$ when $\|\ddot H\|$ is $O(1)$.

## Caveat

IBP requires $\gamma_{G,\nu}(s) \neq 0$ for all $s$ in the integration range — fails at level crossings. The radius of convergence of the asymptotic series generated by repeated IBP is zero: higher-order terms grow as $k!/T^k$, so the expansion is only useful in the large-$T$ limit.
